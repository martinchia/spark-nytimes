In his Wealth Matters column this week, Paul Sullivan writes that recent disclosures of the names of people holding accounts in overseas banks mean that the wealthy are taking their chances if they think those accounts will remain secret. He notes that there is nothing illegal about overseas accounts as long as the Internal Revenue Service knows about them and taxes are paid. A child may be studying abroad, or someone may have a business interest in another country. Or investors may want to open an investment account denominated in a foreign currency to diversify their portfolio. But lawyers and financial advisers say some wealthy people continue to use the accounts to hide money. Growing numbers, in fact, are now going to Singapore and Hong Kong in the wake of the crackdown on private banking in Switzerland. Are there other legitimate reasons for Americans to use offshore accounts?
